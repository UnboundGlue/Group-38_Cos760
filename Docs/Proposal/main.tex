\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
Authorship attribution depends critically on \textbf{text representation}. Classical methods (bag-of-words ({BoW}), term-frequency inverse document frequency ({TF--IDF}), character- and word-level {n}-grams) capture lexical and local sequential cues but may miss writer-specific cues carried by sub-word regularities (e.g.\ morphology and spelling habits) \cite{suman2021authorship,theophilo2021authorship,modupe2022applied}.

\textbf{Subword tokenisation} ({BPE}, {WordPiece}) is now standard in neural {NLP} because it handles rare and variant forms compositionally \cite{sennrich2016neural}; yet it remains unclear, for author discrimination, whether subword inputs outperform strong classical baselines under fair, head-to-head evaluation.

We propose to compare a neural classifier trained on subword sequences to the four traditional feature families above using standard multiclass metrics, and to conduct \textbf{post-hoc explanatory analysis ({SHAP} or {LIME}) and error analysis}, as required by the \textbf{project specification}. The corpus and protocol follow that specification; positioning relative to recent neural attribution work appears in Section~\ref{sec:related} and the bibliography.

\noindent\textbf{Research questions.} \textbf{(RQ1)} Can we learn from the training corpus a \textbf{subword-based representation} (trainable embeddings on {BPE}/{WordPiece} sequences) that supports \textbf{stable multiclass authorship prediction} and is documented for reuse in this setting? \textbf{(RQ2)} How do \textbf{subword-token inputs} compare to \textbf{{BoW}}, \textbf{{TF--IDF}}, \textbf{character {n}-grams}, and \textbf{word {n}-grams} when every pathway follows the same \textbf{train/validation/test} and tuning rules? \textbf{(RQ3)} Does a \textbf{{CNN}--{LSTM}} on subword embeddings \textbf{outperform} the \textbf{validation-best} sparse pipeline on \textbf{held-out} metrics, and which \textbf{subword cues} and \textbf{author pairs} emerge in \textbf{{SHAP}} or \textbf{{LIME}} analyses of \textbf{errors}?

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
Authorship attribution ({AA}) has traditionally relied on sparse, frequency-based feature families, specifically bag-of-words ({BoW}), {TF--IDF}, and word/character {n}-grams, to capture an author's unique ``stylometric fingerprint'' \cite{theophilo2021authorship}. While effective for long-form text, these methods often encounter sparsity and out-of-vocabulary ({OOV}) issues on short-form social posts. Recent work emphasises dense representations via subword tokenisation (e.g.\ {BPE}, {WordPiece}), which decomposes text into morphemic units and can surface sub-lexical stylistic and morphological patterns missed by word-level tokenisation \cite{sennrich2016neural,hossain2025authornet}.

Contemporary work often highlights large transformer ensembles such as \textbf{{AuthorNet}} \cite{hossain2025authornet}, but such systems can be \textbf{computationally heavy} and offer limited transparency about which stylistic markers are emphasised. Studies on microtext attribution (e.g.\ \cite{suman2021authorship,modupe2022subword}) employ deep models yet rarely isolate the \textbf{interaction} of subword embeddings with a hybrid {CNN}--{LSTM} for stylistic discrimination. Much subword-oriented research \cite{toraman2023tokenization,drzik2026morphology} targets general semantic tasks or low-resource language modelling rather than \textbf{individual writing styles} in microtext.

A \textbf{fair, head-to-head} comparison between subword-based neural encoders and the \textbf{full} classical stylometric grid remains under-specified. We evaluate a {CNN}--{LSTM} stack against \textbf{eight} sparse pipelines: four feature families ({BoW}, {TF--IDF}, word {n}-grams, and character {n}-grams) each with \textbf{logistic regression} and \textbf{linear {SVM}}, under identical splits, plus post-hoc \textbf{{SHAP}} or \textbf{{LIME}}. The goal is to test whether subword cues (prefixes, suffixes, morphemic fragments) give a \textbf{stable, interpretable} edge over classical attribution baselines beyond black-box accuracy.

\section{Proposed Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
\noindent\textbf{Dataset.} The COS760 brief points to the publicly shared authorship resource at \url{https://shorturl.at/GJejw}, in line with the social-microtext attribution setting discussed by \cite{theophilo2021authorship,suman2021authorship,modupe2022applied}. We treat the release as \textbf{short, labelled social-style posts} ({CSV}/{JSON}) with \textbf{author labels} mapped to stable ids. After ingest we report \textbf{author count}, \textbf{posts per author}, and length statistics; we expect on the order of \textbf{tens to hundreds of authors} and \textbf{thousands to tens of thousands} of posts unless the release is smaller. We apply stratified \textbf{70/{15}/{15}} splits and a \textbf{minimum-posts-per-author} threshold (e.g.\ ten) before training.

We compare a \textbf{subword {CNN}--{LSTM}} author classifier to \textbf{sparse-feature baselines} under a \textbf{fixed protocol}: shared preprocessing, hyperparameters tuned on validation, \textbf{macro-{F1} for model selection}, and on \textbf{test} \textbf{accuracy}, \textbf{macro} precision/recall/\textbf{{F1}}, \textbf{per-class {F1}}, and a \textbf{confusion matrix}.

\noindent\textbf{Text and subwords.} Posts are cleaned consistently (e.g.\ strip {URLs} and @-mentions, normalise whitespace, \textbf{preserve default casing} for stylometric signal unless we explicitly run a lowercasing ablation; keep punctuation where it carries stylometric signal; drop empties). We train \textbf{{BPE}} (primary) and optionally \textbf{{WordPiece}} \textbf{on the training split only}, with bounded vocabulary and sequence length, then \textbf{encode} all splits with that frozen tokenizer, \textbf{saving the vocabulary} so every run maps text to the same reproducible {ID} sequences.

\noindent\textbf{Neural model.} Embeddings feed \textbf{parallel one-dimensional convolutions} (several widths, \textbf{max-over-time pooling}), then a \textbf{stacked {LSTM}}, \textbf{dropout}, and a \textbf{linear head} over authors. We train with \textbf{Adam}, \textbf{gradient clipping}, and \textbf{cross-entropy}, and select weights by \textbf{validation macro-{F1}} with \textbf{early stopping} for final test scoring.

\noindent\textbf{Encoder--decoder wording.} Course materials sometimes say ``encoder--decoder''; for us that means a representation \textbf{encoder} plus a multinomial \textbf{author-ID head}, not open-ended language generation (no seq2seq decoder).

\noindent\textbf{Baselines.} The same protocol applies to classical pipelines: \textbf{bag-of-words}, \textbf{{TF--IDF}}, \textbf{character {n}-grams}, and \textbf{word {n}-grams}, each fitted \textbf{on train}, with \textbf{logistic regression} and \textbf{linear {SVM}} (\textbf{four extractors} $\times$ \textbf{two classifiers}); tune on \textbf{validation}, evaluate on \textbf{test}.

\noindent\textbf{Explanatory evaluation.} We use \textbf{{SHAP}} or \textbf{{LIME}} on \textbf{subword tokens} (whichever integrates cleanly with our classifier), then summarise errors through the confusion matrix, the \textbf{hardest author pairs}, and \textbf{recurring subword cues}.

\noindent\textbf{Robustness.} \textbf{If} the corpus exposes usable \textbf{metadata} (topic, channel, regime, etc.), we add \textbf{subset or held-out-stratum checks} where the data support it; \textbf{otherwise} we state \textbf{single-corpus scope} and rely on \textbf{per-author {F1}}, full \textbf{confusion matrices}, and \textbf{pairwise confusion} to show \textbf{who is hardest to separate}.

\noindent\textbf{Feasibility.} \textbf{Timeline:} weeks 1--2 cover ingest, cleaning, tokenizer train/encode; weeks 3--4 cover the {CNN}--{LSTM} and the eight sparse baselines; weeks 5--6 cover final test, {SHAP}/{LIME}, light ablations, report and slides. \textbf{Roles:} one member leads data and preprocessing, one the neural model and tokenizer, one sparse baselines plus shared metrics and explainability, with weekly merges. \textbf{Risks:} class imbalance, noisy text, and compute limits; we mitigate with a per-author minimum count, modest hyperparameter search, {CPU}-friendly baselines, and deferring optional analyses if needed.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:eval}
We follow Section~\ref{sec:method}. All models are tuned only on \textbf{validation} ({CNN}--{LSTM} with early stopping on macro-{F1}; each sparse pipeline with its own validation search). The held-out \textbf{test} set is used \textbf{once} for final numbers, under the same split for every system.

We report \textbf{accuracy}, \textbf{macro} precision/recall/\textbf{{F1}}, \textbf{per-class {F1}}, and the \textbf{confusion matrix} via scikit-learn from stored predictions; neural inference runs in batches with \texttt{torch.no\_grad()}. For readability, the main text contrasts {CNN}--{LSTM} with the single sparse run that achieved the highest validation macro-{F1} among the \textbf{eight} configurations (four feature extractors $\times$ logistic regression and linear {SVM}); the remaining seven appear in a compact table or appendix. If time allows, we repeat the same metrics on \textbf{length} bins as a non-core robustness check.

\section{Expected output}
\label{sec:expected}
We expect one final \textbf{test} evaluation for every system and a reproducible \textbf{artefact bundle}: saved subword tokenizer, {CNN}--{LSTM} checkpoint chosen by validation macro-{F1}, eight fitted sparse models, and run configuration (including fixed seeds) with logged metrics in \texttt{results/metrics.json} and artefacts under \texttt{artifacts/}, as in our implementation plan.

The report and slides summarise standard metrics, confusion patterns and hard-to-separate pairs, metadata-based robustness when possible, and \textbf{{SHAP}} or \textbf{{LIME}} on subwords for misclassified examples (with a small contrast sample of correct predictions). \textbf{Either outcome is informative:} a {CNN}--{LSTM} gain on held-out macro-{F1} supports subword-centric representations for this benchmark; if the margin is small, the full baseline grid and explanations still show \textbf{which} author pairs and \textbf{which} subword patterns drive errors.

In sum, we address \textbf{(RQ1)}, \textbf{(RQ2)}, and \textbf{(RQ3)}, linking subword inputs to interpretable author discrimination on the benchmark.
