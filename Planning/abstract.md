Abstract

Authorship attribution — the task of identifying the author of a text based on stylometric features — remains a challenging problem in natural language processing, particularly for short, informal social media content. Traditional feature extraction methods such as Bag-of-Words (BoW), TF-IDF, and character or word n-grams capture surface-level lexical patterns but fail to model the morphological and sub-lexical stylistic cues that distinguish individual writers. This paper investigates the use of subword embedding techniques, specifically Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) and WordPiece tokenisation, as feature representations for authorship attribution on social media datasets. We propose a CNN-LSTM architecture that encodes subword token sequences through parallel convolutional filters of varying kernel sizes, followed by a stacked LSTM to capture sequential dependencies, producing author-discriminative representations. The proposed model is evaluated against four traditional baselines — BoW, TF-IDF, character n-grams, and word n-grams — using macro-F1, precision, recall, and accuracy as performance metrics. To move beyond black-box evaluation, we integrate SHAP and LIME explainability methods to conduct a systematic error analysis, identifying the subword features most responsible for misclassifications and surfacing author-pair confusion patterns. Experiments are conducted on a publicly available social media authorship attribution dataset. Results are expected to demonstrate that subword-aware neural representations outperform traditional feature extraction methods, with explainability analysis providing interpretable insights into the stylometric signals captured by the model.

1. Introduction

The proliferation of user-generated content on social media platforms has created both opportunities and challenges for computational stylometry. Authorship attribution — the problem of inferring the author of an anonymous or disputed text from a set of candidate authors — has long been studied in the context of literary and forensic linguistics [1]. However, the characteristics of social media text, including its brevity, informal register, unconventional spelling, and heavy use of abbreviations and neologisms, render classical authorship attribution methods less effective [2, 3].

Traditional approaches to authorship attribution rely on hand-crafted feature representations derived from the statistical properties of text. Bag-of-Words (BoW) models represent documents as unordered collections of word frequencies, while TF-IDF weighting adjusts for corpus-wide term distributions. Character and word n-gram features capture local sequential patterns and have demonstrated competitive performance on short texts [2]. However, these methods treat vocabulary items as atomic units and are unable to generalise across morphological variants of the same root form. This limitation is particularly acute in social media contexts, where authors frequently employ creative spelling, code-switching, and novel word formations that fall outside any fixed vocabulary.

Neural language models have substantially advanced the state of the art in NLP by learning distributed representations that capture semantic and syntactic regularities from large corpora [1]. A key development enabling this progress is subword tokenisation: rather than operating on whole words or individual characters, subword methods such as Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) [4] and WordPiece [5] decompose words into frequently occurring sub-lexical units. This allows models to represent rare and out-of-vocabulary words compositionally, and to capture morphological patterns — prefixes, suffixes, and stems — that carry stylistic information. Modern transformer-based models such as BERT and GPT leverage subword tokenisation as a foundational component of their input representation [1].

Despite the success of subword representations in general NLP tasks, their specific utility for authorship attribution — a task that depends on fine-grained stylometric signals rather than semantic content — has not been thoroughly investigated. It remains an open question whether subword embeddings can serve as effective stylometric features, and whether the morphological and sub-lexical patterns they encode are sufficiently discriminative to distinguish individual authors, particularly in the low-resource, short-text regime of social media.

This paper addresses this gap by proposing and evaluating a neural authorship attribution system that combines subword tokenisation with a CNN-LSTM encoder-decoder architecture. The CNN component extracts local n-gram-like features from subword embedding sequences using parallel convolutional filters of varying kernel sizes, while the LSTM component models sequential dependencies across the resulting feature representations. This hybrid architecture is motivated by prior work demonstrating the complementary strengths of convolutional and recurrent networks for text classification tasks.

Our contributions are threefold. First, we develop a general-purpose subword vector representation trained from scratch on the target corpus, enabling the model to adapt its vocabulary to the stylistic characteristics of the dataset. Second, we conduct a systematic comparative evaluation of subword-based neural representations against four traditional feature extraction baselines — BoW, TF-IDF, character n-grams, and word n-grams — on a social media authorship attribution benchmark [2, 3]. Third, we integrate post-hoc explainability methods (SHAP and LIME) to perform an error analysis that identifies the subword features most influential in model decisions and characterises the author pairs most susceptible to misclassification, contributing interpretable insights to the stylometric analysis.
